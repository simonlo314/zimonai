Thunderbolt 4 & USB4 guide.

For power users: what these standards actually deliver and what to watch for.

Standards May 29, 2026 8 min read
← Back to Blog

Thunderbolt 4 and USB4 share the same connector (USB-C) but offer different capabilities. For power users connecting docks, eGPUs, and high-bandwidth peripherals, understanding the differences prevents costly mistakes.

USB4 vs USB 3.x — what's the relationship?

USB4 is built on the Thunderbolt 3 protocol foundation. It can tunnel USB data, DisplayPort video, and optionally PCIe. USB4 Gen 3 supports up to 40 Gbps, but many devices implement USB4 Gen 2 (20 Gbps) at lower cost. Not all USB4 implementations are equal.

What Thunderbolt 4 adds over USB4

Thunderbolt 4 is Intel's branding for a minimum baseline that exceeds USB4's minimum specs. Key additions: minimum 32 Gbps PCIe bandwidth (vs optional in USB4), dual display support mandatory, and wake from sleep via connected devices. Thunderbolt 4 ports always support USB Power Delivery, but not all USB4 ports do.

Power delivery on TB4 and USB4

Thunderbolt 4 requires chargers to provide at least 15W via USB PD, with many docks and laptops supporting 60W-100W. USB4 specification allows power delivery but doesn't mandate it. This means a USB4 port on one device might charge your laptop, while another USB4 port on the same device might not. Check the specs carefully.

Cable matters more than you think

Passive USB4/TB4 cables have length limits (typically 0.8m for 40 Gbps, up to 2m at reduced speeds). Active cables with built-in repeaters can go longer but cost more. Using the wrong cable can mean no video output, no charging, or throttled data speeds. Always verify cable ratings match your use case.

Docking station gotchas

Not all docks work with all hosts. Some docks are TB4-only, some work with USB4 but limit features, and some are USB 3.x only despite having a USB-C connector. Before buying a dock, confirm it explicitly supports your laptop's port specification. A dock that "looks compatible" may deliver 50% of the expected capability.
