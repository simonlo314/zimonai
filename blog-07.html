PD vs QC: what's the difference?

Two standards, many misconceptions, and what actually matters for safe charging.

Standards May 15, 2026 7 min read
← Back to Blog

USB Power Delivery (PD) and Qualcomm Quick Charge (QC) are both fast charging standards, but they work differently. Understanding the distinction helps you avoid mismatched chargers and unnecessary risk.

What is USB Power Delivery (PD)?

USB Power Delivery is an open standard managed by the USB Implementers Forum (USB-IF). It supports a wide range of power levels (up to 240W in the latest revision) and is designed to work across devices from phones to laptops. PD chargers negotiate voltage and current dynamically based on what the connected device needs.

What is Qualcomm Quick Charge (QC)?

Quick Charge is Qualcomm's proprietary fast charging technology, tied to devices with Snapdragon processors. It has gone through multiple generations (QC 2.0 through QC 5+), each with its own voltage and current profiles. QC chargers typically work best with QC-compatible devices.

Can you use a PD charger on a QC device?

In most cases, yes. Many modern devices support both standards or fall back to USB PD if QC isn't detected. However, you may not get the fastest possible charging speed if the charger isn't optimized for your specific device. The key point: using a quality PD charger on a QC device is generally safe — it's not a compatibility risk, just a potential performance limitation.

What about using a QC charger on a PD device?

QC chargers that lack USB PD support may not work well with devices that prefer PD. Some older QC chargers also use higher voltages (like 9V or 12V) that some devices don't accept gracefully. If a device expects USB PD and gets only QC signals, it may charge slowly or not at all.

A practical approach

For most modern devices, USB PD is the safer default choice — it's widely supported, standardized, and future-proof. If you have multiple devices with different requirements, a quality multi-port PD charger that covers your range is often simpler and safer than juggling protocol-specific chargers.
