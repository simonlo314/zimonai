Travel charging safety.

Airports, hotels, and foreign sockets: how to charge safely when traveling.

Tips June 5, 2026 7 min read
← Back to Blog

Traveling introduces unfamiliar power environments: different voltages, outlet types, and shared charging stations. A few precautions help you avoid device damage, unexpected power cuts, or worse.

Voltage matters — most of the world is different

North America uses 110-120V, while most of the world (Europe, Asia, South America, Africa, Oceania) uses 220-240V. Most modern USB chargers are dual-voltage (100-240V), so they'll work anywhere. But older devices, some laptop chargers, and certainly anything with a non-USB charging port needs a voltage converter, not just an adapter.

Airport USB ports: convenience vs risk

Airport charging stations are convenient but carry Juice Jacking risk — malicious USB ports that can install malware or steal data. This is more theoretical than common in practice, but if you want to be safe, use a USB data blocker (a small, inexpensive adapter that passes only power, not data) or carry a wall charger and plug directly into an outlet.

Hotel chargers: check the wattage

Some hotel USB ports are limited to 5W (slow) or have poorly regulated power. More concerning: some hotels have USB ports built into outlets or alarm clocks that are rarely updated and may have aging components. If your device needs higher wattage, use your own charger plugged into a wall socket. Save the USB ports for low-power devices like headphones.

Power strips and adapters: watch for the daisy chain

Using a travel adapter into a power strip into an extension cord is tempting when outlets are scarce, but this compounds resistance and heat risk. If you're running multiple high-power devices, plug them directly into separate wall outlets where possible. Overloaded power strips are a real fire risk.

What to pack

A good travel kit: your own quality USB charger with sufficient wattage for your devices, a USB data blocker for public ports, a universal adapter (not just a passive plug converter), and ideally a compact power meter that shows voltage and amperage. This lets you verify the power quality before plugging in expensive gear.
